Step 1 — Reviewing The RFQ
Before anything else, verify the RFQ is complete and accurate. A missed detail here costs hours later.
✅ What to do
  • Check if the PN and Description are matching — the part number must correspond to the correct description
  • Check the QTY (Quantity) requested by the customer
  • Check the ALT (Alternate PNs) if given — valid if description matches the original PN
  • Check the Comments for special conditions, deadlines, or customer notes
  • Check if marked AOG (Aircraft on Ground) — highest priority, handle immediately
💡 Tips
💡
ALT Verification
Sometimes suppliers offer an ALT (alternate PN). To verify it's valid: check if the description is the same as the original. Same description = valid alternate. Different = not compatible — do not accept it.
ℹ️
AOG Priority
AOG = Aircraft on Ground. The aircraft cannot fly until the part arrives. Every hour of delay costs the airline significantly. Always escalate AOG requests immediately — they bypass normal queue.
Step 2 — Creating The Template
Build a clean, professional email draft before reaching out to any supplier. Getting this right saves you from corrections later.
✅ What to do
  • Create a separate draft for each PN (or one combined if multiple PNs go to same suppliers)
  • Verify QTY matches exactly what the customer requested
  • Verify Description matches the PN exactly
  • Set Subject: RFQ PN [0123FA2] for single / RFQ PNs [A / B] for multiple
  • Check font and size — some email clients alter formatting
  • Remove all extra spaces and formatting artifacts before sending
📨 What to send
📨 RFQ Email
Subject: RFQ PN [0123FA2] Hello [Name], Please provide your best price quote and stock availability for the following item: 1 PN# [0123FA2] / [SENSOR - LOW FLOW] Thank you. Best regards
💡 Tips
⚠️
Multiple PNs — Use Supplier View
When working multiple PNs at once, paste them all in Partbase, ILS, or Locatory and enable Supplier View / Bulk Entry. It shows which suppliers have which PNs — then edit your template to include only what that specific supplier has.
Step 3 — PN Search
Find stock across all platforms before reaching out. Cast a wide net, then narrow down to the best options.
✅ What to do
  • Search the PN on the Internal Google Sheet first — avoid duplicating work already done
  • Search Main Platforms: ILS, Partbase, Locatory, B2B, Stockmarket
  • Search Secondary Platforms: Satair, AJW, Proponent
  • Ask the Manufacturer directly (use ChatGPT to find their contact email)
  • Search Google: "PN aviation supplier" or "PN for sale aircraft"
  • Ask Co-workers or check invoice product history — someone may have sourced it before
🖱️ Where to click
  • Google Sheets → Internal tracker → search PN in the sheet
  • Open ILS / Partbase / Locatory / B2B / Stockmarket → search PN
  • Use the Search & Reminders tab to find direct contact emails
  • Multi-PN: use Supplier View or Bulk Entry mode on Partbase/ILS
💡 Tips
💡
Full Pipeline Check
If you sent quotes a month ago and got nothing back, revisit each platform and filter by upload date — new stock may have appeared. Also re-run Google; new supplier pages get indexed over time.
💡
Market Price Research
Check price using: quotes you've collected, Partbase Market Price button, Stockmarket Price Search, Proponent, AJW, or Satair. For New (NE) parts these platforms are especially useful.
🛠️
GetProspect Extension
GetProspect is a browser extension that extracts professional emails from LinkedIn profiles. Use it when you need a direct contact at a supplier or manufacturer — especially for OEM outreach.
✈️
Aviation Alphabet on the Phone
When calling a supplier to spell a PN or email, always use the aviation phonetic alphabet (Alpha, Bravo, Charlie, Delta…). Avoids costly confusion between similar-sounding letters like B/D, M/N, or S/F.
⚠️
AR — Last Resort Option
As Removed (AR) can only be offered after finding no certified stock anywhere. AR parts have a removal tag only — not a certification — since they haven't been sent to a repair shop yet.
Step 4 — Sending The RFQs
Send requests carefully. Every duplicate or missing detail wastes time and damages your reputation with suppliers.
✅ What to do
  • Include the supplier's name — never send a generic "Dear Sir/Madam"
  • Use the Direct Contact (from Search tab) and CC the general sales email
  • Check for duplicates: search @domainname.com — not the full email address
  • Always include the QTY — suppliers will not quote without it
  • Edit Subject: RFQ PN [X] single / RFQ PNs [X / Y] multiple
  • PartBase clients may post offers themselves — do NOT send them an RFQ, they are buyers

Avoid Duplicates — Search by Domain

⚠️
Don't Blow Up the Market

Step 5 — Quote Treatment
When a supplier responds, validate every field carefully before taking any action.
✅ What to verify
  • PN matches your RFQ exactly
  • Description matches (if an ALT is offered — verify same description)
  • Condition: OH, SV, AR, NE, BT, OR…
  • QTY available
  • Stock (available now) vs Lead Time (needs repair first — no cert yet)
  • Tag Src — who repaired it / which shop released it
  • Tag Date — when was it last repaired / certified
  • Outright or Exchange?
📖 Key Terms
🔄
Outright vs Exchange
Outright: You pay money, you get the part. Simple purchase.
Exchange: You give your AR core part + cash → receive a repaired/OH unit. Two types: Flat Rate Exchange and Standard Exchange. Never accept without manager approval.
⏳
Lead Time
Part is NOT yet repaired — no certification yet. It may come with trace documents (PPW) and NIS documentation. Always inform the client. Ask the repair shop for their TAT (Turn Around Time) to give the client a realistic delivery date.
❓
OR — On Request
Supplier doesn't know the condition yet. They must request the part from a repair shop first. Condition (OH, SV…) will be confirmed after inspection. Treat like lead time — no cert yet.
🔖
C/C — Condition/Condition (e.g. RP/OH)
Current condition / what it becomes after certification. e.g. RP/OH means repaired now, will become OH after cert. Treat as uncertain until the actual cert is received.
📦
QTY on Cert vs Quote
Cert says 10, quote says 2 — this is normal. The shop repaired 10 units total, sold 8, has 2 remaining — which is what they quoted you for.
⚠️ Important Notes
⚠️
Add All Quotes — Even Bad Ones
Record every quote in the database — even if sold, overpriced, or wrong condition. Future requests for the same PN benefit from this data, and revised quotes need the history.
💡
When to Ask for Certification
Request the cert only when: price is good + tag date is recent + condition is right + part is in stock. Don't ask from every supplier — it wastes their time and yours, and can put you in an awkward position.
Step 6 — Certification Review
The certification is the most critical document in the entire process. Every single field must match. One wrong date can invalidate the deal.
✅ What to check
  • PN on cert matches the PN on your RFQ
  • Description on cert matches
  • Serial Number (SN) matches what was quoted
  • Condition matches (OH, SV, NE…)
  • Block 12 & Block 5 — no supplier name or logo visible
  • Block 14a/14b (FAA 8130-3) or Block 13a (EASA Form 1) — release authority confirmed
  • Date format: MM/DD/YYYY — verify month and day are NOT swapped
  • Note the Tag Date — newer is always better
  • No supplier signatures, stamps, or marks anywhere on the cert
📖 Certification Types
🇺🇸
FAA Form 8130-3
US airworthiness approval tag. Block 14a = Approved per 14 CFR Part 43. A single checkmark in Block 14a = New part (never installed). Block 14b = approved under other regulations.
🇪🇺
EASA Form 1
European equivalent of the FAA 8130. Block 13a = Release statement. Required for all parts entering European aviation maintenance operations.
📋
Dual Release
Both FAA and EASA approval on one cert. Written in Block 12 at the very bottom line. Most flexible cert type — accepted by both US and European operators.
📋
ATA 106
ATA Specification 106 — industry-standard certification for serviceable aircraft parts issued by US repair stations. Widely accepted alongside the 8130.
📋
CofC — Certificate of Conformance
Manufacturer's declaration that the part meets its approved design specifications. Used for new surplus (NE/NOS) parts that don't have an 8130. Confirms the part was manufactured to spec.
⚠️ Important Notes
⚠️
Date Format is Critical
The format must be MM/DD/YYYY. If you're unsure, Google the date shown on the cert. A swapped month/day causes rejection at customs, compliance, and the client level.
⚠️
Supplier Name in Block 12 or 5
If you see a supplier's name in Block 12 or 5, the cert must be rejected or corrected by the issuing shop. Submitting it with a supplier name visible is a compliance violation.
⚠️
Remove All Unwanted Paperwork
Before submitting a cert package to a client, remove any irrelevant documents. Only approved trace documents + the cert should go to the client.
Step 7 — Trace & PPW
Trace documentation proves the complete history of a part. This section is ready for you to fill in manually with your specific requirements and scenarios.
📋 What is Trace / PPW?
📁
Trace / PPW — Paperwork
All documents that follow a part's history: removal tags, work orders, previous certifications, shop findings reports, overhaul records. The more complete the trace, the more verifiable the part's airworthiness history.
🏷️
AR Part = Removal Tag, Not Certification
An As Removed part has a Removal Tag only — not a certification. This is because it has not yet been sent to a repair shop. Once repaired, it receives a proper cert (8130, EASA Form 1, etc.).
⏳
Lead Time Parts
No cert yet — the part hasn't been repaired. May include trace documents and NIS (New In Service) paperwork showing the part's origin and prior use history.
📋 Trace Items to Check
  • Removal tag (for AR parts) — confirms removal from a specific aircraft
  • Work orders / shop findings — record of what was inspected or repaired
  • Previous 8130 or EASA Form 1 — prior certification history
  • Overhaul records — TAT details, shop name, completion date
  • Life-limited part records — total time in service, cycle counts, limits
  • Last/Next Hydro — hydrostatic test dates for hydraulic components
  • PIN Life — for brakes: remaining % of brake life (70–100% is ideal)
  • BT (Bench Test) — functional test on the ground without full overhaul
📖 Trace Terms Explained
💧
Last / Next Hydro
For hydraulic components — a hydrostatic test checks structural integrity under pressure. Last Hydro = date of last test. Next Hydro = when the next test is due / expiry date. If Next Hydro is expired, the part cannot legally be used.
🔩
PIN Life
Used for aircraft brakes. Shows how much usable brake material remains, expressed as a percentage. 70–100% is generally acceptable. Below 70% requires careful evaluation and explicit client approval before proceeding.
🧪
BT — Bench Test
A functional check performed on the ground without full disassembly or overhaul. Confirms the part operates correctly. BT condition = serviceable and tested, but not fully overhauled to new tolerances.
⏱️
TAT — Turn Around Time
How long the repair shop takes to complete work on the part — from receiving it to releasing it certified. Always ask for TAT on lead time parts so you can give the client a realistic delivery estimate.

Step 8 — Sending The Reminders
Follow up with suppliers who haven't replied. The default follow-up window is 3 hours after the initial RFQ was sent.
✅ What to do
  • Filter your sent emails — identify which suppliers have NOT replied
  • Reply to the original RFQ email — do not compose a new email thread
  • Keep the reminder short, polite, and professional
  • Include the Sales email in CC and address the supplier by name

⏰

Step 9 — Picking The Offer
Compare all quotes received and select the best one using a strict priority order. Never compromise the Tag Date for a lower price.
✅ Priority Order (top to bottom)
  • Tag Date — most recent certification date wins over everything
  • Condition — OH > SV > AR. Never swap conditions without explicit client consent
  • Stock vs Lead Time — in-stock is always preferred over lead time
  • Price — lowest price among otherwise equivalent offers
  • EXW Location — closest to buyer reduces shipping cost and transit time
💰 Total Cost
💰
Total Cost = Price + Shipping + Processing + Your Margin
Price — what the supplier charges for the part
Shipping — customer typically pays; for restricted countries we ship to our warehouse first, then forward to buyer
Processing fees — packaging, handling, admin overhead
Time / Margin — your profit for the sourcing work

Never evaluate an offer on price alone. Calculate the full total cost before deciding.
📍 EXW Explained
📍
EXW = Ex Works = FOB = Incoterm — Where the Part Ships From
EXW is the physical location where the part will be shipped from. To find it: check supplier's website, ask ChatGPT, or ask the supplier directly.

Important: supplier office ≠ warehouse. Some suppliers have multiple warehouses worldwide. A US supplier may store parts in Kenya to serve African clients — EXW would be Kenya, not USA. Always verify actual shipping location, especially for urgent AOG orders.
💡
Make Offer / Target Price
Before making a target price offer to a supplier, always consult the manager or director. If you offer too high and they accept, you are locked in at that price. Always offer below market price. Once they accept, you can proceed to place the PO.
⚠️ Important Notes
⚠️
No Exchange Without Manager Approval
Exchange offers require management sign-off before committing. Never agree to exchange terms independently.
⚠️
Confirm Availability Before Picking
Before selecting an offer, call or email the supplier to confirm the part is still available. Aviation listings go stale very quickly.
Step 10 — Invoice Process
Fill every field from the CERTIFICATION — never from the quote. One wrong field means the client rejects the invoice and you start over.
✅ What to do
  • Open the invoice/quote system
  • Fill Description from the CERT (not the supplier quote)
  • Fill SN from the CERT
  • Set Tag Type: MFG 8130 / CofC / ATA106 / DUAL FAA / EASA FORM-1
  • Set Tag Src: repair station name exactly as shown on cert
  • Set Tag Date from cert — verify format is MM/DD/YYYY
  • Set Condition as listed on the cert
  • Set Warranty: 6 MONTHS (standard)
  • Set EXW: actual warehouse location
  • CC: Houssam and Moe on every initial email
  • Set invoice status to "Initial Email"

⚠️

Verify the client name and company are correct before submitting. Sending a quote to the wrong client is a serious error.
Step 11 — Client Follow-Up
Follow up with the client 48 hours after sending the quote. Keeps the deal alive and shows professionalism.
✅ What to do
  • Wait 48 hours after sending the quote before following up
  • Reply to the original quote email — do not start a new thread
  • Remove your own email from the To: field — set To: to the client only
  • Keep the message short and professional

Terminology & Glossary
Every abbreviation and term used in aviation parts sourcing, explained clearly.
PN
Part Number — unique identifier for an aircraft component
RFQ
Request for Quotation — formal request to suppliers for price and availability
OEM
Original Equipment Manufacturer — the company that originally designed and built the part
AOG
Aircraft on Ground — aircraft cannot fly until part is received. Highest priority in aviation.
OH
Overhauled — fully disassembled, inspected, repaired to new tolerances, and certified
SV
Serviceable — inspected and cleared for use, but not fully overhauled
AR
As Removed — removed from an aircraft, not yet inspected or certified. Has a removal tag only.
NE / NEW
New — never installed on any aircraft. Single check in Block 14a of FAA Form 8130-3.
NOS
New Old Stock — new part, manufactured but stored for extended period, never installed
RP
Repaired — part has been repaired but may not be fully overhauled to new tolerances
BT
Bench Test / Bench Tested — functionally tested on the ground without full overhaul
OR
On Request — supplier doesn't know condition yet; must send for inspection first
C/C
Condition/Condition e.g. RP/OH — current condition / what it becomes after certification
Outright
Purchase type: you pay money, you receive the part. No exchange involved.
Exchange
You send your AR core part + cash and receive a repaired unit. Types: Flat Rate and Standard Exchange.
EXW
Ex Works — Incoterm indicating where the part physically ships from (supplier warehouse location)
FOB
Free On Board — seller delivers to port/airport, buyer handles all costs after that point
DDP
Delivered Duty Paid — seller delivers to buyer's door, all costs included
TAT
Turn Around Time — how long a repair shop takes to complete work and release the part certified
PO
Purchase Order — official document confirming an order placed with a supplier
8130 / FAA F1
FAA Form 8130-3 — US airworthiness approval tag. Single check in Block 14a = New part.
EASA F1
EASA Form 1 — European airworthiness release certificate, equivalent to the FAA 8130-3
Dual Release
Both FAA and EASA approval on one cert. Written in Block 12 bottom line. Accepted globally.
ATA 106
ATA Spec 106 — industry-standard cert for serviceable parts from US repair stations
CofC
Certificate of Conformance — manufacturer's declaration that part meets approved design spec
BER
Beyond Economical Repair — part is too damaged to repair cost-effectively
MRO
Maintenance, Repair & Overhaul — facilities that perform aircraft maintenance and part repairs
PMA
Parts Manufacturer Approval — FAA-approved alternative parts meeting the same airworthiness standards as OEM
TSO
Technical Standard Order — FAA minimum performance standard for specific parts or materials
Cage Code
5-character NATO code that identifies a specific manufacturer or supplier
Incoterms
International trade terms defining who pays for shipping, insurance, customs (EXW, FOB, DDP…)
PIN Life
Remaining usable life of a brake assembly, expressed as a %. 70–100% is generally acceptable.
Hydro
Hydrostatic test for hydraulic components. Last Hydro = last test date. Next Hydro = expiry date.
PPW
Paperwork / Trace — the full history documentation package of a part
NIS
New In Service — documentation for a part entering service for the very first time
GetProspect
Browser extension that extracts professional email addresses from LinkedIn profiles
CC
Carbon Copy — additional email recipients copied on the message

Pro Tips
Hard-earned knowledge from real sourcing work. Read these regularly — they save hours and prevent costly mistakes.
📦 Sourcing & Market
💡
Total Cost Thinking
Never evaluate an offer by part price alone. Total cost = supplier price + shipping + processing fees + your margin. For restricted countries, we ship to our warehouse first then forward to the buyer — factor this additional leg into the cost.
💡
ALT Verification
Suppliers sometimes offer an ALT (alternate PN) instead of the exact one you need. The only way to verify it's a valid alternate: confirm the description matches the original PN. Same description = compatible. Different description = not the same part, do not accept.
💡
Full Pipeline Check
Sent quotes a month ago with no replies? Go back to the platforms, filter by upload date — new stock may have appeared since. Also re-run Google searches, new supplier pages get indexed regularly.
💡
Add All Quotes — Even Sold / Bad Ones
Record every quote in the database regardless of outcome. Future requests for the same PN benefit from this data. Revised quotes always need the pricing history. Quotes marked as sold still provide market price reference.
💡
QTY on Cert vs Quote
Cert says 10, quote says 2 — this is completely normal. The repair shop repaired 10 units total, sold 8 of them, has 2 remaining. Your quote is for those 2 available units.

💰 Pricing & Negotiation
💡
Market Price Research
Determine market price using: quotes you've collected (compare the range), Partbase Market Price button, Stockmarket Price Search, Proponent, AJW, or Satair. For New (NE) parts, OEM platforms like Proponent and Satair are the best reference.
⚠️
Make Offer / Target Price
Always consult the manager or director before making a target price offer to any supplier. If you offer too high and they accept immediately, you are locked in at that price. Always start below market price. Get manager approval first, then place the PO only after they accept.
💡
Quote-to-Sales Ratio
Avoid requesting quotes without genuine purchase intent. Suppliers track this ratio. A poor ratio leads to being deprioritized or receiving less favorable pricing. Build trusted relationships with your core supplier network first.
🌍 EXW & Logistics
📍
Finding the Real EXW Location
To find EXW: check supplier's website for warehouse locations, ask ChatGPT with the supplier name, or ask the supplier directly. Key insight: supplier office ≠ warehouse location. Some suppliers have warehouses across multiple continents — the part ships from wherever it's physically stored.
🌍
Multi-Warehouse Example
A US-headquartered supplier may store parts in a Kenya warehouse to supply African airlines. The EXW would be Kenya, not USA. Always verify actual shipping origin before confirming delivery timelines, especially for urgent AOG situations.
